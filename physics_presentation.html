CONSTRAINED MEMBRANE PHYSICS

Core Mathematical Principles of Energy Conservation and Variational Shape Minimization

1. Conservation of Energy

If the paddle frame is locked in place (the stationary boundary condition \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{paddle}} = \mathbf{0}\)), the coupled ball-membrane system acts as a perfectly conservative physical system.

The total mechanical energy—representing the sum of the ball's kinetic energy, gravitational potential energy, and the membrane's elastic potential energy—remains strictly constant over time:

$$E_{\text{total}} = K + U_g + U_e = \text{constant}$$

Where the individual energy components are defined mathematically as:

  • Kinetic Energy (\(K\)) of the ball:
    \(K = \frac{1}{2} m \|\mathbf{v}\|^2\)
  • Gravitational Potential Energy (\(U_g\)) of the ball:
    \(U_g = m g z_b\)
  • Elastic Potential Energy (\(U_e\)) stored in the stretched membrane:
    \(U_e = \frac{U_{e,\text{centered}}(z_b)}{1 - (x_b^2 + y_b^2)}\)

    Note: The denominator \(1 - (x_b^2 + y_b^2)\) scales the potential energy based on the ball's off-center distance, naturally representing the increased stiffness of the membrane near the clamped boundary.

2. Variational Membrane Shape & Dirichlet Principle

The shape of the circular massless membrane \(u(x, y)\) is governed by the **Dirichlet Principle** of variational calculus: the membrane must deform to the exact profile that minimizes its total elastic potential energy under the physical constraint of the contacting sphere.

Energy Minimization Objective:

$$\text{Minimize } E_{\text{elastic}} = \frac{1}{2} T \iint_{\Omega} \|\nabla u\|^2 \, dx \, dy$$

This variational principle divides the membrane shape into two distinct, coupled zones:

  • Inside the Contact Area (\(d \le r_c\)):
    The membrane is kinematically constrained to conform perfectly to the spherical bottom of the ball of radius \(R\):
    $$u(x, y) = z_b - \sqrt{R^2 - r_{\text{ball}}^2}$$
  • Outside the Contact Area (\(d > r_c\)):
    Free from any external load, the membrane minimizes its elastic (Dirichlet) energy. By the Euler-Lagrange equations, this corresponds to solving the Laplace Equation:
    $$\nabla^2 u = 0 \implies u(d) = A \ln(d)$$

    This yields the exact logarithmic funnel profile of a massless sheet.

  • The Contact Boundary (\(d = r_c\)):
    The contact radius \(r_c\) and coefficient \(A\) are solved analytically by matching both the heights (continuity) and the slopes (smoothness) at the perimeter where the two regions meet, yielding the absolute global minimum of the system's energy.

3. Lagrangian Principle & Virtual Work: State Transition (\(t \to t + \Delta t\))

The dynamic step-by-step transition of the coupled ball-membrane system from the current scene (state \(t\)) to the next scene (state \(t + \Delta t\)) is governed by a dual-regime framework combining Lagrangian Dynamics for the ball and the Principle of Virtual Work for the membrane.

The Coupled Lagrangian (\(L\)):

$$L = T - V = \frac{1}{2} m \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 - \Big( m g z_b + U_e(x_b, y_b, z_b) \Big)$$

On each integration time step, the system transitions from the current scene to the next through the following sequence:

  • 1. Static Equilibrium of the Membrane (Virtual Work):
    Because the membrane is modeled as massless, it has zero inertia and remains in instantaneous static equilibrium. For any virtual displacement \(\delta u\) compatible with the boundary constraints, the virtual work done by internal tension and the ball's contact force \(\mathbf{F}_c\) must vanish:
    $$\delta W = T \iint_{\Omega} \nabla u \cdot \nabla (\delta u) \, dx \, dy - \mathbf{F}_c \cdot \delta \mathbf{r}_b = 0$$
    This is equivalent to solving the exact static shape of the membrane \(u(x, y, t)\) based on the ball's current position \(\mathbf{r}_b(t)\) at time \(t\).
  • 2. Force Evaluation (Euler-Lagrange):
    The membrane's shape exerts a conservative restoring force \(\mathbf{F}_{\text{membrane}}(t)\) back onto the ball center, which is derived exactly from the spatial gradient of our coupled potential:
    $$\mathbf{F}_{\text{membrane}}(t) = -\nabla_{xyz} U_e(x_b, y_b, z_b) = \begin{bmatrix} - \frac{2 x_b}{(1 - r_b^2)^2} U_{e,\text{centered}} \\ - \frac{2 y_b}{(1 - r_b^2)^2} U_{e,\text{centered}} \\ \frac{F_{z,\text{centered}}}{1 - r_b^2} \end{bmatrix}$$
  • 3. Symplectic State Update:
    This conservative force vector is fed into the ball's Euler-Lagrange equations of motion:
    $$\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} = 0 \implies m \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_b = \mathbf{F}_{\text{gravity}} + \mathbf{F}_{\text{membrane}}(t)$$
    This is integrated over the sub-step \(\Delta t_s\) to find the velocity and position of the next scene:
    $$\mathbf{v}(t + \Delta t_s) = \mathbf{v}(t) + \frac{\mathbf{F}(t)}{m} \Delta t_s$$
    $$\mathbf{r}_b(t + \Delta t_s) = \mathbf{r}_b(t) + \mathbf{v}(t + \Delta t_s) \Delta t_s$$
  • 4. Seamless Mesh Rendering (Smooth Blending):
    In the next scene, the membrane's 3D mesh coordinates are updated to \(u(x, y, t + \Delta t_s)\) using our direction-aware blending function to maintain perfect boundary continuity, ready for the next physics tick.

4. Other Rigid-Body Constraints: (Almost) Elastic Collisions

In addition to the flexible membrane, the ball is constrained laterally and vertically by rigid boundary structures. When the ball impacts these boundaries, we apply Newton's Law of Restitution for rigid-body collisions.

The Velocity Reflection Law:

$$\mathbf{v}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{v} - (1 + e)(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n})\mathbf{n}$$

The rigid constraints and collision physics are constructed as follows:

  • 1. The Concentric Cylinder Boundary (\(R_{\text{cyl}} = 1.45\text{ m}\)):
    The outer boundary of the playground is a fixed vertical cylinder. A collision is triggered exactly when the outer boundary (edge) of the sphere touches the cylinder wall:
    $$r_b = \sqrt{x_b^2 + y_b^2} \ge R_{\text{cyl}} - R_{\text{ball}} = 0.95\text{ m}$$
  • 2. The Circular Frame Ring (\(R_{\text{frame}} = 1.0\text{ m}\)):
    The clamped rigid circular ring holding the membrane acts as a 3D wireframe constraint in the \(z = 0\) plane. A collision occurs when the 3D distance \(d_{\text{ring}}\) from the ball center to the closest point on the ring is less than or equal to the ball's radius:
    $$d_{\text{ring}} = \sqrt{(r_{xy} - R_{\text{frame}})^2 + z_b^2} \le R_{\text{ball}} = 0.5\text{ m}$$
  • 3. Restitution & Damping for Game Feel (\(e = 0.95\)):
    While we use perfect elasticity (\(e = 1.0\)) in mathematical verification scripts to demonstrate ideal energy conservation, the playable game engines use a calibrated restitution coefficient of \(e = 0.95\):
    $$\mathbf{v}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{v} - 1.95(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n})\mathbf{n}$$

    This 5% kinetic energy dissipation simulates realistic material damping, stabilizes the numerical time-stepping integration, and prevents the ball from entering unplayable high-speed bouncing cycles against the walls.

  • 4. Penetration Correction (Clamping):
    To prevent visual wall-clipping due to finite time-step sizes, we project/clamp the ball's position exactly back to the contact boundary:
    $$\mathbf{r}_b = \mathbf{p}_{\text{closest}} + R_{\text{ball}} \mathbf{n}$$

    Crucially, this projection is executed only when an active collision is taking place (\(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n} < 0\)). This prevents the ball from getting "glued" to boundaries when it is moving away, allowing crisp, clean bounces.
